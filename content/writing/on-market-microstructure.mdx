The bid-ask spread is the first thing a new trader notices and the last thing
most of them truly understand. It looks like a transaction tax. It is not.

## The three-component decomposition

Glosten and Harris (1988) decompose the spread into three parts:

1. **Order processing costs** — the cost of running the matching engine,
   clearing and settling the trade, paying the humans involved. These are
   boring and declining toward zero as technology improves.

2. **Inventory risk** — market makers accumulate positions they don't
   necessarily want. A dealer who has just sold you 10,000 shares of something
   is now short, and the world might move against her before she can unwind.
   The spread compensates for this risk.

3. **Adverse selection** — this is the interesting one.

## Why adverse selection is different

The first two components are about cost and risk in a world where both sides
of the trade have roughly equal information. Adverse selection is what happens
when they don't.

A market maker who quotes a two-sided market doesn't know whether the person
hitting her bid is a noise trader liquidating a position to buy a car, or a
hedge fund that read a 10-K and knows something she doesn't. She can't tell.
So she widens the spread just enough that the profits from the uninformed flow
cover the losses from the informed flow.

This is the Glosten-Milgrom (1985) insight, and it has a disturbing implication:
**the spread is determined by the worst-case counterparty, not the average one.**

## The market design consequence

If you want tighter spreads — more liquid markets — you either need to reduce
the fraction of informed traders (hard; information is hard to suppress) or
reduce the harm they cause to market makers (easier; this is what post-trade
transparency rules attempt to do).

Payment for order flow, IEX's "speed bump," the debate over maker-taker pricing:
all of these are downstream of this one observation about who is really on the
other side of the trade.
