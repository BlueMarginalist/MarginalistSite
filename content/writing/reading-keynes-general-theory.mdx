These are working notes, not a review. I am reading Keynes slowly and trying
to figure out what he actually said versus what people claim he said.

## The two classical postulates (Chapter 2)

Keynes opens by attacking what he calls the classical theory of employment.
The two postulates:

1. The wage equals the marginal product of labor.
2. The utility of the wage when a given volume of labor is employed equals the
   marginal disutility of that amount of employment.

The first he largely accepts. The second he rejects, and the rejection is the
whole point.

Postulate 2 implies that unemployment is always voluntary — workers are off
their labor supply curve by choice. If people are unemployed, it is because
the real wage is above their reservation wage and they prefer leisure. Policy
can't help; just wait for wages to adjust.

Keynes's argument is that workers bargain over *nominal* wages, not real wages,
and they can't unilaterally cut their real wage by accepting a lower nominal
wage (because prices will fall too). The labor market does not self-clear in
the relevant timeframe.

## The consumption function (Chapter 8–10, teased earlier)

The consumption function $C = C(Y)$ with $0 < C'(Y) < 1$ is the key behavioral
equation. The marginal propensity to consume (MPC) being less than 1 is what
creates the multiplier.

This seems obvious now. In 1936 it was not: the classical view was roughly that
income would be fully spent or saved and then invested, so demand was never
deficient. Keynes's contribution was to say that saving decisions and investment
decisions are made by different people for different reasons, and there is no
mechanism that guarantees they coincide.

## What "aggregate demand" actually means

Keynes defines aggregate demand somewhat differently than modern textbooks. For
him, the "aggregate demand function" $Z = \phi(N)$ maps employment $N$ to the
expected proceeds that entrepreneurs require to find it worthwhile to employ $N$
workers. The "aggregate supply function" $D = f(N)$ gives expected proceeds as
a function of employment.

Equilibrium is where $D = Z$, which he calls the "effective demand."

The modern $AD/AS$ diagram is a translation of this into price-output space that
came later. It loses some of Keynes's emphasis on *expectations* — his $D$ is
what entrepreneurs *expect*, not what consumers *spend*.
